# Abstract

Porous cellulose acetate membranes fabricated via solvent-template evaporation-induced phase separation (EIPS) are promising for passive radiative cooling due to their high solar reflectance. We develop a mechanistic mathematical model to optimize and control the membrane’s light reflection performance, quantified by the pore area ratio in SEM images. The model integrates five sequential process stages – solvent evaporation, nonsolvent and polymer precipitation, Brownian diffusion of nascent droplets, droplet coalescence into larger droplets, and growth of large droplets – each grounded in physical theory. Evaporation kinetics are described by a first-order model incorporating DMF vapor pressure (via the Antoine equation) and a linear humidity suppression term. Phase separation of sulfolane (nonsolvent) and polymer is captured by mass balance constraints using solubility limits. Brownian motion of sulfolane droplets is modeled via the Stokes-Einstein diffusion coefficient, with solution viscosity enhanced by precipitated polymer according to Einstein’s effective viscosity relation. Droplet collision kinetics are formulated for both small-small collisions (creating new large droplets) and small-large collisions (growth of existing large droplets). The final pore area ratio is assumed proportional to the total mass of large droplets formed by the end of evaporation.

Using provided experimental data, we estimate model parameters and validate the model against measured pore area ratios for various temperatures (30–50 °C), relative humidities (50–90%), and solid contents (6–10%). The calibrated model shows good agreement with experiment, accurately reproducing the non-monotonic effects of temperature and humidity and the strong influence of polymer content on pore formation. In particular, the model explains the existence of an optimal evaporation rate at intermediate temperature (~40 °C) that maximizes pore area and reflectance, as a result of balancing rapid phase separation with sufficient time for droplet growth. Through numerical optimization of the model, we identify favorable fabrication conditions: moderate evaporation temperature (~40 °C), high humidity (~85–90%), and low polymer solid content (~5–6%), which together maximize the membrane pore area ratio. A sensitivity analysis confirms temperature and polymer fraction as the dominant factors controlling pore area, while ambient humidity plays a secondary role. A revised model neglecting humidity effects yields a similar optimal temperature and solid content but underestimates achievable pore area, highlighting humidity’s subtle yet important influence. Overall, the model provides a comprehensive quantitative understanding of how processing conditions affect porous microstructure and optical performance. It enables rational design of experiments to tune pore architecture for maximum reflectivity. We discuss the model’s assumptions and limitations – including idealized kinetic laws and neglect of certain interactions – and suggest improvements such as refined viscosity models and droplet size distributions. Nevertheless, the model successfully links process parameters to membrane structure and performance, offering valuable guidance for optimizing radiative cooling materials.

# Introduction

Passive radiative cooling materials have attracted increasing attention as a sustainable strategy to combat global warming by emitting heat to the cold sky. Porous polymer membranes with high solar reflectance are promising candidates, as their microstructured pores scatter sunlight and minimize solar absorption. **Cellulose acetate porous membranes** prepared by solvent-template-assisted evaporation-induced phase separation (EIPS) are one such material, demonstrating good solar reflectance and cooling performance. However, current fabrication of these porous films faces challenges: the microstructure (pore size and distribution) is difficult to precisely control, and the relationships between fabrication conditions, microstructure, and optical performance remain poorly understood. To design high-reflectance membranes, it is critical to quantitatively understand how processing parameters affect pore formation and the resulting reflectance.

In this work, we develop a **mathematical modeling framework** for the EIPS fabrication of porous cellulose acetate membranes, to link fabrication conditions (temperature, humidity, composition) with the membrane’s pore area ratio and reflectance. We address the multi-step mechanism of pore formation: solvent (DMF) evaporation, precipitation of nonsolvent (sulfolane) and polymer (cellulose acetate), Brownian motion and coalescence of liquid droplets, and the solidification of these droplets into pores. By integrating thermodynamic and kinetic theories (Antoine equation for vapor pressure, Stokes-Einstein diffusion, Einstein viscosity, collision dynamics), we construct a **theoretical model** describing pore area ratio as a function of fabrication conditions.

We then calibrate this model using experimental data (Attachment 2) for various temperatures (30, 40, 50 °C), relative humidities (50%, 70%, 90%), and polymer solid contents (6%, 8%, 10%). Model parameters are estimated based on physical reasoning and by fitting the model to the data, ensuring the model reproduces observed trends. Using the validated model, we determine the optimal fabrication conditions that maximize the pore area ratio (and thus reflectance). Finally, we perform a **sensitivity analysis** to identify the influence of each factor and consider a simplified model if certain factors are negligible, comparing its predictions to the full model. This stepwise approach addresses all aspects of the problem: deriving a mechanism-based model, applying it to find unknown parameters, optimizing conditions, and analyzing factor effects.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. We first restate the problem and list our modeling assumptions and notation. We then present a detailed mechanism analysis and theoretical model derivation for each stage of the process. Next, we estimate model parameters using the given data and solve the model to predict pore area ratios, comparing with experiments. We then use the model to optimize fabrication conditions for maximum pore area ratio. A sensitivity analysis and discussion of influential factors (with a revised model if appropriate) are provided, followed by an evaluation of the model’s performance and limitations. We conclude with key findings and implications for designing high-reflectance porous membranes.

# Problem Restatement

**Objective:** Develop a self-contained mathematical model for the **optimization and control of light reflection performance** (pore area ratio) of porous cellulose acetate membranes produced by the solvent-template EIPS method, and use it to address four tasks: (1) formulate a theoretical model for pore area ratio based on the five key mechanistic steps of the fabrication process; (2) determine unknown model parameters using the provided experimental data and validate the model; (3) find the optimal fabrication conditions (temperature, humidity, solid content) to maximize the pore area ratio; and (4) perform an influence factor analysis on the pore area ratio using the data, possibly refine the model, re-optimize, and compare results.

**System Description:** In the EIPS process (Attachment 1), a homogeneous solution of cellulose acetate (polymer), N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF, volatile solvent), and sulfolane (nonvolatile nonsolvent template) is cast. As DMF evaporates, the solution becomes supersaturated in sulfolane and polymer, causing phase separation. *Five stages* occur sequentially: 1) **DMF evaporation** at the solution surface (governed by temperature and ambient humidity); 2) **Precipitation of sulfolane and polymer** as DMF leaves (due to reduced solubility); 3) **Brownian diffusion of small sulfolane-rich liquid droplets** that nucleate upon phase separation; 4) **Collisions and coalescence of small droplets into larger droplets**; and 5) **Growth of large droplets** by absorbing smaller ones. After evaporation, the film is solidified (e.g. by quenching or drying), and the remaining DMF and sulfolane droplets are removed (e.g. by washing), leaving behind a porous polymer matrix. Large sulfolane droplets produce large pores that dominate the membrane’s solar reflectance, whereas small droplets yield small pores with negligible contribution. Thus, the **pore area ratio** (total area of pores in an SEM image divided by total image area) is determined primarily by the population of large droplets at the end of evaporation. Maximizing pore area ratio should correspond to maximizing the mass/volume of large droplets formed during the process. The fabrication conditions of interest are: **evaporation temperature (T)**, **ambient relative humidity (H)**, and **polymer solid content (SC)** in the initial solution (polymer mass as a percentage of total DMF+sulfolane mass).

**Modeling Goals:** We aim to derive an analytical/dynamical model $f(T, H, SC; \mathbf{k})$ for the pore area ratio (with $\mathbf{k}$ a set of model parameters) by performing a mechanism-based analysis of each process stage. We will identify the physical meaning of all parameters and justify any simplifications made for tractability. Using experimental data (Attachment 2), we will fit the model to find numerical values of unknown parameters and assess the model’s accuracy. We will then use the calibrated model to determine the optimal $T$, $H$, and $SC$ that maximize the pore area ratio (hence reflectance). Finally, we will analyze the influence of each factor on pore area via statistical means and consider a simplified model if appropriate, re-optimizing and comparing with the full model’s recommendations.

# Assumptions and Notation

To make the problem tractable, we adopt the following **assumptions** (with justifications) and define key notation:

- **Uniform Film and Negligible Convection:** The evaporation of DMF is assumed to occur from a flat, uniform film surface of constant area (no edge effects), with no external airflow. Thus, evaporation is controlled by diffusion into quiescent air and depends only on vapor pressure and concentration of DMF. This simplifies modeling the evaporation rate.
- **Ideal Solution and Additivity of Volumes:** We treat the liquid mixture (DMF + sulfolane + dissolved polymer) as ideal and volume-additive. The total solution volume $V_{\text{sol}}$ is taken as the sum of the pure-component volumes (thus $V_{\text{sol}}(t)=\frac{m_D}{\rho_D}+\frac{m_S}{\rho_S}+\frac{m_C}{\rho_C}$, with $m_D, m_S, m_C$ the masses of DMF, sulfolane, and polymer, and $\rho_i$ their densities). This ignores any excess volume or strong interactions in solution, which is reasonable given the relatively dilute polymer and similar polar nature of DMF and sulfolane.
- **Selective Solubility:** We assume DMF is the only significant solvent for both sulfolane and cellulose acetate, whereas sulfolane’s ability to dissolve the polymer is negligible. This is supported by Hansen solubility parameters: the polymer is much more compatible with DMF (Hansen distance 8.76) than with sulfolane (distance 12.36). Thus, sulfolane acts effectively as a nonsolvent in the presence of polymer. Ignoring any direct sulfolane–polymer dissolution simplifies the precipitation model and focuses on DMF’s role, with minimal loss of accuracy.
- **Local Equilibrium Solubility for Precipitation:** At any time during evaporation, the dissolved fractions of sulfolane and polymer remain at equilibrium saturation concentrations in the remaining DMF. In other words, any excess beyond DMF’s solvating capacity precipitates immediately. This assumes precipitation kinetics are fast relative to the evaporation-driven concentration change (reasonable given rapid nucleation observed in such phase separations). It allows a straightforward mass-balance determination of precipitated mass.
- **Instantaneous Nucleation of Droplets:** Precipitated sulfolane is assumed to form **suspended small spherical droplets** immediately upon exceeding solubility (phase separation), rather than, say, a continuous separate phase. These droplets are initially uniformly distributed throughout the solution and are taken to have a characteristic initial radius $r_0$ (on the order of microns). Each droplet consists primarily of sulfolane (and any associated minor DMF) and is considered a Brownian particle in the liquid. We neglect any gravitational settling or stratification of droplets, given their small size and the short timescales (Brownian motion dominates over sedimentation for micron-scale droplets).
- **Negligible Coalescence Barrier:** We assume that whenever two sulfolane droplets collide (by Brownian motion), they coalesce into a larger droplet. This ignores any kinetic barrier (e.g. droplet stabilization by polymer at the interface). Given the polymer is not a surfactant and phase separation yields liquid-liquid demixing, coalescence is likely favorable. Thus, the droplet size evolution can be modeled by coagulation kinetics driven by collisions.
- **Pore Formation:** We assume that **large droplets** (resulting from multiple coalescence events) correspond to final *macropores* that contribute to the pore area ratio, whereas **small droplets** that never coalesce remain as *micropores* that do not contribute significantly to reflectance. We take a threshold (implicitly defined in our model by the coalescence process itself) that separates “large” vs “small” droplets. In practice, this means we count any droplet that has undergone at least one collision as a “large” droplet (a reasonable proxy, as even one coalescence roughly doubles a droplet’s diameter). Small initial droplets that never collide produce pores too small to affect reflectance and are ignored in the pore area ratio.
- **Optically Thin Film Assumption:** The pore area ratio measured from surface SEM images is assumed to represent the overall porosity and directly correlate with total solar reflectance. We do not explicitly model the optical properties (e.g. Mie scattering), relying on the empirical correlation that higher area fraction of pores (especially large pores) yields higher reflectance. Therefore, maximizing pore area ratio is taken as equivalent to optimizing optical performance.
- **Isothermal Process:** The evaporation is assumed isothermal at the set temperature $T$. Evaporative cooling or latent heat effects are neglected, assuming the experiment controls the substrate temperature or the heat exchange is sufficient to maintain constant temperature. This allows us to treat $T$ as a fixed parameter affecting vapor pressure and diffusion.
- **Negligible Airflow and Spatial Gradients:** We assume uniform conditions throughout the film (well-mixed liquid) and just above it (no concentration gradient of DMF in the air beyond what is encapsulated in the evaporation rate law). This is implicit in using a lumped kinetic model for evaporation rather than solving diffusion in air. Likewise, we neglect any concentration gradients within the film – effectively assuming **uniform evaporation** (justified for thin films where diffusion of DMF inside the film is fast compared to evaporation, or if the film thickness is small).

**Notation:** For clarity, we summarize key variables and parameters:

- $m_D(t), m_S(t), m_C(t)$ – masses of DMF, sulfolane, and cellulose acetate in the system at time $t$. Initial masses are $m_D(0)$, $m_S(0)$, $m_C(0)$. We treat initial DMF and sulfolane as known (from the 4:1 weight ratio: e.g. 24 g DMF, 6 g sulfolane【8†skip-7†】) and $m_C(0)$ determined by the solid content (e.g. 6% SC means $m_C(0)=0.06(m_D(0)+m_S(0))$).
- $T$ – evaporation temperature (°C, converted to K in formulas as needed).
- $H$ – ambient relative humidity (%). We use $H$ in fractional form (e.g. 0.5 for 50%) in equations.
- $\mathcal{S}_S, \mathcal{S}_C$ – DMF’s solubility capacity for sulfolane and cellulose (defined as mass fraction or ratio). Specifically, we define $\mathcal{S}_S$ such that $m_D \cdot \mathcal{S}_S$ is the maximum mass of sulfolane that can be dissolved in DMF mass $m_D$. Similarly $\mathcal{S}_C$ for polymer in DMF. These may be treated as constants (at given temperature) to be determined.
- $m_{S,\text{diss}}(t)$, $m_{C,\text{diss}}(t)$ – masses of sulfolane and polymer dissolved in DMF at time $t$. $m_{S,\text{prec}}(t), m_{C,\text{prec}}(t)$ will denote the precipitated (undissolved) mass of sulfolane and polymer respectively at time $t$. At $t=0$, all material is initially dissolved ($m_{S,\text{prec}}=m_{C,\text{prec}}=0$). As $m_D$ decreases, $m_{S,\text{prec}}$ and $m_{C,\text{prec}}$ increase whenever $m_S$ or $m_C$ exceed DMF’s dissolving capacity.
- $n(t)$ – number density of *small* sulfolane droplets in the liquid (count per unit volume) at time $t$. We will use related quantities $N(t)$ for total number of small droplets in the system volume, and $m_{S,\text{small}}(t), m_{S,\text{large}}(t)$ for mass of sulfolane in small vs large droplets. Initially, $N(0)=0$ (no droplets) and as sulfolane precipitates, it enters the small-droplet form ($m_{S,\text{small}}$). Collisions will transfer mass from the small-droplet pool to the large-droplet pool ($m_{S,\text{large}}$). The total sulfolane mass is conserved: $m_S = m_{S,\text{diss}} + m_{S,\text{small}} + m_{S,\text{large}}$ at any time.
- $r_0$ – characteristic radius of newly formed small droplets. We treat all small droplets as having roughly this initial size (monodisperse assumption for the small-droplet population). For large droplets, an effective radius $R$ can be considered for an equivalent single large droplet of mass $m_{S,\text{large}}$ (with $R \propto m_{S,\text{large}}^{1/3}$), but in modeling we primarily track mass rather than radius.
- $D$ – diffusion coefficient of small droplets in the liquid (via Brownian motion). $\eta$ – dynamic viscosity of the liquid medium. These are functions of temperature and polymer concentration.
- $k_1, k_h$ – empirical parameters in the evaporation rate model (defined in the next section). $k_1$ sets the base evaporation rate constant and $k_h$ represents the fractional reduction in evaporation rate per unit humidity (with $0 \le k_h \le 1$).
- $k_{\text{coll}}, k_{\text{abs}}$ – kinetic coefficients for droplet coalescence: $k_{\text{coll}}$ for small–small collisions (coagulation) and $k_{\text{abs}}$ for small–large collisions (absorption). These will appear in the rate equations for $m_{S,\text{large}}(t)$ formation and are to be determined by fitting to data.
- $\mathbf{k}$ – a vector denoting the set of all model parameters to be fitted (e.g. $\mathbf{k}=(k_1, k_h, \mathcal{S}*S, \mathcal{S}*C, k*{\text{coll}}, k*{\text{abs}}, \ldots)$).
- $\Phi$ – pore area ratio (porosity) of the final membrane, in %. In our model, $\Phi$ will be taken proportional to the mass (or volume) fraction of sulfolane that ends up in large droplets at the end of evaporation. For direct comparison to experimental values, we compute $\Phi = (m_{S,\text{large}}(t_{\text{end}})/m_S(0)) \times 100%$, assuming each large droplet produces a pore of equal volume (and small pores contribute negligibly).

Next, we proceed to derive the model equations for each stage of the process, using these assumptions and notations.

# Mechanism Analysis and Theoretical Modeling

**1. DMF Evaporation Kinetics:** The evaporation of DMF is the driving force initiating phase separation. We model the DMF mass loss with a first-order kinetic law that accounts for the volatile concentration and vapor–liquid equilibrium. Let $m_D(t)$ be the DMF mass at time $t$. The evaporation rate (mass flux) can be expressed as:
$$
\frac{dm_D}{dt} = -k_1 \,\theta_D(t)\,\big(P_{\text{sat}}(T) - P_{\text{amb}}\big)\,f(H) , \tag{1}
$$
where $\theta_D = \frac{m_D}{V_{\text{sol}}}$ is the mass concentration of DMF in the mixture (proportional to DMF volume fraction), $P_{\text{sat}}(T)$ is the saturated vapor pressure of DMF at temperature $T$, $P_{\text{amb}}$ is the ambient partial pressure of DMF in the air, and $f(H)$ is a humidity correction factor. We set $P_{\text{amb}}\approx0$ (assuming initially no DMF in air and good ventilation) for simplicity. The form of Eq. (1) reflects: (i) evaporation rate is proportional to the volatile’s concentration in the solution (more DMF yields faster evaporation) and (ii) it increases with the vapor pressure driving force $P_{\text{sat}}-P_{\text{amb}}$. Here $k_1$ is a rate constant (with appropriate units) encapsulating mass transfer coefficients and system geometry.

To compute $P_{\text{sat}}(T)$ of DMF, we use the **Antoine equation**:
$$
\log_{10} P_{\text{sat}}(\text{Pa}) = A - \frac{B}{T(\text{K}) + C} , \tag{2}
$$
with Antoine constants $A, B, C$ for DMF. Using data from the literature, for example, $A=3.93068$, $B=1337.716$, $C=-82.648$ (valid $T\approx303$–363 K), one finds $P_{\text{sat}} \approx7.3\times10^2$ Pa at $30^\circ$C and $2.3\times10^3$ Pa at $50^\circ$C (signifying a roughly three-fold increase over this range). This substantial rise in vapor pressure with temperature explains the strong temperature dependence of evaporation rate.

**Humidity Effect:** High ambient humidity slows the evaporation of any solvent by reducing the driving force for vaporization (since humid air contains more vapor molecules, leaving less partial pressure “space” for other vapors). Empirical studies show evaporation rate is approximately linear in relative humidity at constant temperature. We incorporate this via $f(H) = 1 - k_h,H$, where $0 \le k_h \le 1$ is a coefficient describing humidity’s suppression effect. We take $H$ as a fraction (e.g. 0.5 for 50% RH). Thus, Eq. (1) becomes:
$$
\frac{dm_D}{dt} = -k_1\,\frac{m_D}{V_{\text{sol}}}\,P_{\text{sat}}(T)\,\big(1 - k_h H\big) . \tag{3}
$$
When $H=0$ (dry air), $f(H)=1$ and the rate is maximal; when $H=1$ (100% RH), the term $(1-k_h)$ indicates how much evaporation is reduced (if $k_h=1$, evaporation would essentially stop at 100% RH, a limiting case). We will determine $k_h$ from data – typically moderate-volatility solvents have $k_h$ in the 0.5–1 range (strong humidity dependence), whereas low-volatility solvents are less affected by humidity. DMF, with intermediate volatility, is expected to have a significant humidity effect; we anticipate $k_h$ closer to 1, meaning near-saturation humidity almost halts DMF evaporation, consistent with qualitative observations.

For modeling convenience, we neglect the slight decrease of surface area as the film dries and any temperature variation – $T$ is fixed, and $k_1$ is assumed constant during the process. Integration of Eq. (3) (coupled with volume changes in $\theta_D$) will give $m_D(t)$, but it generally requires numerical solution. However, key behaviors can be deduced: higher $T$ exponentially accelerates evaporation (via $P_{\text{sat}}$), while higher $H$ slows it linearly. This evaporation rate will crucially influence how quickly the solution becomes supersaturated, and thus the timing of precipitation events.

**2. Sulfolane and Polymer Precipitation (Phase Separation):** As DMF evaporates, the remaining volume shrinks and the concentrations of sulfolane and polymer increase. Eventually, DMF can no longer keep all sulfolane and polymer dissolved. We denote $\mathcal{S}_S$ as DMF’s solubility capacity for sulfolane (g sulfolane per g DMF) and $\mathcal{S}_C$ for polymer in DMF. These are treated as constants at the operating temperature (assuming slight temperature variation of solubility is negligible in our narrow range). Mechanistically, $\mathcal{S}_S$ and $\mathcal{S}_C$ represent where liquid-liquid and liquid-solid phase separation, respectively, begin.

Using the assumption of instantaneous equilibrium, at any time:

- The maximum sulfolane that can be dissolved in $m_D$ of DMF is $m_D \mathcal{S}_S$. If the total sulfolane $m_S$ exceeds this, the excess precipitates as a separate sulfolane-rich phase (droplets). Thus, the precipitated sulfolane mass is:

$$
m_{S,\text{prec}}(t) = \max\{\,0,\;m_S - \mathcal{S}_S\,m_D(t)\,\} . \tag{4a}
$$

If $m_S \le \mathcal{S}*S m_D$, all sulfolane remains dissolved ($m*{S,\text{prec}}=0$). Initially, $m_D(0)$ is large and $\mathcal{S}_S m_D(0)$ likely exceeds $m_S(0)$; but once enough DMF evaporates such that $m_D < m_S/\mathcal{S}_S$, sulfolane starts to precipitate.

- Similarly, polymer begins to precipitate when $m_D$ can no longer dissolve $m_C$. The precipitated polymer mass is:

$$
m_{C,\text{prec}}(t) = \max\{\,0,\;m_C - \mathcal{S}_C\,m_D(t)\,\} . \tag{4b}
$$

If $m_C \le \mathcal{S}_C m_D$, polymer stays in solution; if $m_C > \mathcal{S}_C m_D$, the excess polymer crashes out as a solid (or gel) phase.

These equations define the “crossover” points for phase separation. For example, in one experimental case (30 °C, 50% RH, 6% SC), initial $m_D\approx24$ g, $m_S=6$ g, $m_C=1.8$ g. If we assume $\mathcal{S}_S \approx 0.25$ (i.e. 1 g DMF can dissolve 0.25 g sulfolane – a value chosen so that initial DMF is just saturated with 6 g sulfolane) and $\mathcal{S}_C \approx 0.15$ (1 g DMF dissolves 0.15 g polymer), then initially $m_S = \mathcal{S}_S m_D$ (sulfolane right at saturation) and $m_C < \mathcal{S}_C m_D$ (polymer fully dissolved). As $m_D$ drops, sulfolane will start precipitating immediately (since it was at the threshold), while polymer might not precipitate until $m_D$ falls below $m_C/\mathcal{S}_C \approx 12$ g. This staggered precipitation is expected: sulfolane (nonsolvent) comes out first, forming liquid droplets, and polymer precipitation (solid network) occurs later when DMF is sufficiently lost. Our model naturally captures that sequence through Eqs. (4). The simplifying assumption of independent solubility (sulfolane precipitation does not change polymer solubility and vice versa) is justified by the weak interaction between polymer and sulfolane.

It is important to note that once polymer begins precipitating, the solution’s viscosity will increase sharply (as addressed in the next section), which in turn will slow down the diffusion and coalescence of droplets. Thus, polymer precipitation often “locks in” the microstructure. Our model will reflect that by coupling $m_{C,\text{prec}}$ to viscosity. Also, polymer precipitating may form a matrix that limits how far droplets can move, but we assume precipitated polymer is still somewhat mobile (like a gel) until DMF is almost completely gone.

**3. Brownian Motion of Small Droplets:** When sulfolane precipitates as tiny droplets, these droplets undergo random Brownian motion in the liquid mixture due to thermal fluctuations. Brownian diffusion is crucial as it brings droplets together to collide and coalesce. We quantify it by the **Stokes-Einstein equation**, which gives the diffusion coefficient $D$ for a spherical particle of radius $r$ in a fluid of viscosity $\eta$:
$$
D = \frac{k_B\,T}{6\pi\,\eta\,r}, \tag{5}
$$
where $k_B$ is Boltzmann’s constant and $T$ is absolute temperature (in K). Equation (5) indicates that higher temperature (more thermal energy) increases $D$, whereas higher viscosity or larger particle size decreases $D$. In our context, as $T$ rises from 30 °C to 50 °C, $D$ increases moderately (due to $T$ in numerator), but more importantly, viscosity $\eta$ can change dramatically during the process, strongly affecting $D$.

**Effective Viscosity of the Suspension:** Initially, the viscosity $\eta_0$ of the DMF/sulfolane/polymer solution is low (close to DMF’s viscosity, since polymer content is small and sulfolane is liquid). As polymer precipitates, it forms a dispersed solid phase (or highly swollen gel), which increases the viscosity of the remaining liquid mixture. We assume the primary contributor to viscosity rise is the precipitated polymer (solid particles/fibrils) rather than the sulfolane droplets. Sulfolane droplets are a second liquid phase; at small volume fractions they behave somewhat like particles, but their influence is secondary compared to polymer because polymer can create network-like structures.

For a dilute suspension of solid particles, Einstein’s formula gives the relative viscosity: $\eta/\eta_0 = 1 + 2.5,\phi$ (with $\phi$ the volume fraction of particles). For higher concentrations, more advanced models are needed (e.g. Batchelor’s second term, or empirical formulas). In our data, polymer solid content is up to 10%, which by volume might be on the order of a few percent (since polymer density $\rho_C\approx1.3$ g/mL). Thus, using the Einstein linear term is a first approximation. We write:
$$
\eta(t) = \eta_0(T) \Big(1 + 2.5\,\phi_C(t)\Big) , \tag{6}
$$
where $\eta_0(T)$ is the viscosity of the polymer–solvent solution before polymer precipitation (temperature-dependent, e.g. DMF’s viscosity $\approx0.8$ mPa·s at 25 °C and $\approx0.5$ mPa·s at 50 °C) and $\phi_C(t)$ is the volume fraction of precipitated polymer in the mixture. We estimate $\phi_C = \frac{V_{C,\text{prec}}}{V_{\text{sol}}-V_{C,\text{prec}}} \approx \frac{m_{C,\text{prec}}/\rho_C}{m_D/\rho_D + m_S/\rho_S}$ (since precipitated polymer’s volume is removed from the liquid). This increases from 0 up to potentially $\sim!0.1$ as polymer precipitates. If $\phi_C$ approaches or exceeds the dilute limit, Eq. (6) may under-predict $\eta$; one could instead use an exponential form like $\eta/\eta_0 = \exp(\alpha \phi_C)$. Indeed, Cheng and Law (2003) proposed an exponential viscosity formula valid for higher concentrations. In our simulations we will monitor $\phi_C$ and, if needed, apply an exponential correction for large $\phi_C$. For analytical derivation, we proceed with Eq. (6) for simplicity, acknowledging it may slightly understate viscosity at the very end of evaporation when polymer volume fraction is high.

Using Eqs. (5) and (6), we can evaluate $D(t)$ as DMF evaporates. At the start, $\eta \approx \eta_0$ (low, on the order of $10^{-3}$ Pa·s), so $D$ for a 1 µm droplet at 313 K is ~$2\times10^{-13}$ m²/s, meaning a droplet’s root-mean-square displacement in 1 s is $\sqrt{6D,1}\approx \mathcal{O}(10^{-6})$ m, i.e. about its own size – indicating noticeable mobility. Toward the end, if $\phi_C$ becomes large, $\eta$ could increase by an order of magnitude or more, drastically reducing $D$. Thus, time is of the essence: most coalescence should happen before $\eta$ skyrockets.

**Brownian Droplet Velocity:** While Brownian motion is inherently random, we can characterize an average “diffusive velocity” of droplets to plug into collision rate formulas. Over a short time $\Delta t$, a droplet’s displacement magnitude is $\sim\sqrt{6D\Delta t}$ (3D diffusion). One can define an *effective velocity* $v_b$ as displacement/time, e.g. $v_b \approx \sqrt{\frac{6D}{\Delta t}}$ for some small $\Delta t$ that characterizes the ballistic regime. In fact, on very short time scales below the momentum relaxation time of the particle, the motion is ballistic (not random). However, for our purposes, we simply assume the average random velocity scales with $\sqrt{D}$ or $D$ in a way that can be absorbed into collision coefficients. Following a simplifying approach, we take $v_b(t)$ proportional to the diffusion speed:

$v_b(t) = C_b \, D(t)/r_0,$

with $C_b$ a proportionality constant of order unity. (This form has the right units: $D/r_0$ has units of velocity, and effectively assumes a droplet diffuses a distance on the order of its diameter per some characteristic time.) We will absorb $C_b$ into the collision rate constants later, so we do not need its explicit value. Importantly, $v_b(t)$ increases with $T$ (via $D$) and decreases with rising $\eta$ or $r_0$. So higher temperature or smaller droplets move faster, while a more viscous medium slows droplets down, consistent with physical expectation.

**4. Droplet Collision and Coalescence (Small–Small collisions):** The suspended sulfolane droplets collide with each other by random motion. We model collisions using kinetic theory analogies. Consider two small droplets of radius $r$ moving with average relative speed $\sim \sqrt{2}v_b$. The collision cross-section (for coalescence) is roughly $\sigma = \pi (2r)^2 = 4\pi r^2$ (treating them as hard spheres of diameter $2r$). The collision frequency for a given droplet in a sea of others of number density $n$ is $n \sigma v_{\text{rel}}$. Accounting for both droplets moving, a standard result is:
$$
f_{\text{coll}} = 2\sqrt{2}\,\pi\,r^2\,n\,v_b , \tag{7}
$$
where $f_{\text{coll}}$ is the collision frequency *per droplet*. The $\sqrt{2}$ factor arises from random thermal motion (Maxwellian velocity distribution). Equation (7) essentially matches the form given in Attachment 1. We can convert this to the total number of collisions per unit volume per unit time: $R_{\text{coll}} = \frac{1}{2} n \cdot f_{\text{coll}}$ (the $\frac{1}{2}$ avoids double-counting pairs). Substituting $f_{\text{coll}}$ yields $R_{\text{coll}} = \sqrt{2},\pi,r^2,n^2,v_b$.

Each such collision merges two small droplets into one larger droplet. We define any droplet formed by coalescence as a “large droplet.” Thus, every collision event essentially transfers the mass of two small droplets from the small-droplet population to the large-droplet population. Let the mass of one small droplet be $m_{d0}$ (assumed roughly constant for initial droplets of radius $r_0$). Then each collision removes $2m_{d0}$ from $m_{S,\text{small}}$ and adds that to $m_{S,\text{large}}$. The rate of change of large-droplet mass due to small–small collisions can then be written as:
$$
\frac{dm_{S,\text{large}}}{dt}\Big|_{\text{coll}} = 2 m_{d0}\,R_{\text{coll}}\;V_{\text{sol}} = 2 m_{d0}\,\sqrt{2}\,\pi\,r^2\,n^2\,v_b\,V_{\text{sol}} , \tag{8}
$$
where $V_{\text{sol}}$ is the current total volume of the mixture (so $n^2 V_{\text{sol}}^2 = N_{\text{small}}^2$, the square of total number of small droplets). It is convenient to express this in terms of masses and concentrations rather than number density. Noting $n = \frac{N_{\text{small}}}{V_{\text{sol}}}$ and $m_{S,\text{small}} = N_{\text{small}} m_{d0}$, we can simplify Eq. (8) to a form proportional to the square of small-droplet mass or concentration. We introduce a collision-coalescence coefficient $k_{\text{coll}}$ to capture all the constants:
$$
\frac{dm_{S,\text{large}}}{dt}\Big|_{\text{coll}} = k_{\text{coll}}\, \frac{m_{S,\text{small}}^2}{V_{\text{sol}}} , \tag{9}
$$
where $k_{\text{coll}} \sim \frac{\sqrt{2},\pi,r^2}{m_{d0}V_{\text{sol}}} v_b$ times some constant factors. Since $m_{d0}$ relates to $r$ by $m_{d0} = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \rho_S$, we can simplify the dimensional analysis, but ultimately $k_{\text{coll}}$ will be determined by fitting the model to the observed degree of coalescence. Equation (9) captures the expected behavior: coalescence rate increases with the square of small-droplet mass fraction (i.e. more small droplets → more frequent collisions) and with droplet velocity $v_b$ (faster motion → more collisions). As evaporation proceeds, $m_{S,\text{small}}$ initially rises (sulfolane precipitating) then falls as collisions convert small to large; $V_{\text{sol}}$ decreases as DMF evaporates. Solving Eq. (9) along with the evaporation and diffusion equations will give us the evolution of $m_{S,\text{large}}(t)$ from this mechanism.

**5. Growth of Large Droplets (Small–Large collisions):** Once large droplets (of significantly bigger size) form, they can grow further by absorbing remaining small droplets. A moving small droplet that collides with a stationary large droplet will coalesce into it, increasing the large droplet’s mass. We assume large droplets are much fewer in number and essentially suspended without moving far (their sedimentation is negligible, but we treat their Brownian movement as minimal due to larger size and perhaps getting trapped in viscous polymer matrix). Thus, we treat large droplets as quasi-static targets and small droplets as mobile.

For a single large droplet of surface area $S$ (approximately $4\pi R^2$ for radius $R$) in a field of small droplets with number density $n$ moving with velocity $v_b$, the flux of small droplets onto the large droplet is $n v_{\perp} S$, where $v_{\perp}$ is the component of the small-droplet velocity perpendicular to the surface. Assuming isotropic motion, on average $v_{\perp} \approx \frac{1}{4}v_b$ (only those droplets moving toward the surface within a certain solid angle will hit it). Thus, collisions per unit time $\approx \frac{1}{4} n v_b S$. Each such collision adds the mass $m_{d0}$ of one small droplet to the large droplet. Now, if there are multiple large droplets, we sum over all. Alternatively, consider all large droplets collectively having total surface area $S_{\text{tot}}$. The total mass absorption rate by all large droplets is approximately:
$$
 \frac{dm_{S,\text{large}}}{dt}\Big|_{\text{abs}} = m_{d0}\,\frac{1}{4} n\,v_b\, S_{\text{tot}}\,V_{\text{sol}} . \tag{10}
$$


We expect $S_{\text{tot}}$ to be proportional to the total large-droplet mass $m_{S,\text{large}}$. For simplicity, if we assume the large droplets are all roughly the same size (or we lump them into one equivalent droplet), then $S_{\text{tot}} \propto (m_{S,\text{large}})^{2/3}$ (since surface area $\propto R^2 \propto (m)^{2/3}$ for a spherical drop of mass $m$). A more accurate expression would include the number of large droplets, but lacking detailed distribution data, we incorporate any dependence on large-droplet count into an effective coefficient. Thus we set $S_{\text{tot}} = \kappa,(m_{S,\text{large}}/\rho_S)^{2/3}$, where $\kappa$ is a proportionality constant related to the number of large droplets (if one large droplet, $\kappa = (6\pi)^{2/3}$; if many, $\kappa$ would be larger as total area adds up).

Substituting into Eq. (10) and grouping constants, we get a form:
$$
\frac{dm_{S,\text{large}}}{dt}\Big|_{\text{abs}} = k_{\text{abs}}\, m_{S,\text{large}}^{2/3}\, \frac{m_{S,\text{small}}}{V_{\text{sol}}} \, v_b , \tag{11}
$$


where $k_{\text{abs}}$ is another fitting parameter. This relation states that the growth rate of large-droplet mass is proportional to: the concentration of small droplets ($n \sim m_{S,\text{small}}/V_{\text{sol}}$), the flux $v_b$ of small droplets, and the total surface area of large droplets ($\propto m_{S,\text{large}}^{2/3}$). It is consistent with the qualitative statement in Attachment 1 that large-droplet growth rate is proportional to the collision frequency of small droplets onto them. Initially, when $m_{S,\text{large}}$ is small, this absorption term is negligible; but once a sizable large-droplet mass exists, this term can dominate (since $m_{S,\text{large}}^{2/3}$ grows as large droplets get bigger, presenting more surface to capture small ones).

**Link to Pore Area Ratio:** By the end of evaporation ($t_{\text{end}}$ when DMF is essentially gone), we have a certain mass $m_{S,\text{large}}(t_{\text{end}})$ of sulfolane in large droplets, and $m_{S,\text{small}}(t_{\text{end}})$ remaining in small droplets (if any). In the subsequent solidification and washing steps, all sulfolane is removed, leaving voids where the droplets were. Based on our assumption, pores from large droplets correspond to the measurable pores affecting reflectance, whereas pores from small droplets are too tiny to count. Therefore, the **pore area ratio** $\Phi$ (in %) can be related to the volume (or mass) fraction of sulfolane that had coalesced into large droplets:
$$
\Phi \approx \frac{m_{S,\text{large}}(t_{\text{end}})}{m_{S}(0)} \times 100\% , \tag{12}
$$
assuming densities are similar such that mass fraction approximates area fraction. We might also scale by the polymer/sulfolane density ratio and any shrinking of the polymer matrix, but those effects are minor compared to the large differences between scenarios. Equation (12) is in line with the statement that final pore area ratio is proportional to large-droplet mass.

In summary, combining the pieces: we have a set of coupled equations – Eq. (3) for $m_D(t)$ evaporation, Eqs. (4) for precipitation (which effectively give $m_{S,\text{prec}}, m_{C,\text{prec}}$ as functions of $m_D$), and Eqs. (9) and (11) for the evolution of large-droplet mass $m_{S,\text{large}}(t)$ due to collisions and absorption. The small-droplet mass $m_{S,\text{small}}(t)$ is determined by the difference $m_{S,\text{prec}} - m_{S,\text{large}}$. These equations depend on time implicitly through $m_D(t)$ and $v_b(t)$ (which in turn depends on $D(t)$ and thus on $\eta(t)$ as given by Eq. (6)). The full system does not yield a simple closed-form solution; we will solve it numerically for given parameters.

However, qualitatively the model explains why an intermediate evaporation rate might maximize $\Phi$: if evaporation is too slow (low $T$, high $H$), DMF leaves gradually – sulfolane precipitates late and polymer may remain dissolved until the end, yielding fewer, smaller droplets (some sulfolane might remain dissolved until very low DMF, then precipitate as many small droplets but with not enough time to coalesce before polymer comes out). If evaporation is too fast (high $T$, low $H$), polymer precipitates very early (because $m_D$ drops quickly) locking the structure and freezing many droplets in small size before they can collide – plus the Brownian motion time is cut short. At an optimal moderate rate, there is a window where lots of droplets form but polymer has not yet gelled the system, and Brownian motion is active enough to coalesce droplets into larger ones, boosting $m_{S,\text{large}}$. Our model captures these effects via the interplay of $m_D(t)$ (evaporation kinetics) and the time-dependent viscosity in $v_b(t)$ and collision rates.

# Parameter Estimation and Model Solution

We determine the model parameters by fitting the theoretical model to the experimental data provided (Attachment 2), which consist of pore area ratio measurements under 3×3×3 combinations of temperature $T$, relative humidity $H$, and solid content $SC$. The parameters to estimate include: $(k_1, k_h)$ in the evaporation model, $(\mathcal{S}*S, \mathcal{S}*C)$ for solubility limits, and $(k*{\text{coll}}, k*{\text{abs}})$ for the coalescence kinetics (the initial droplet size $r_0$ is implicitly contained in these). Some of these can be approximated from known data or initial conditions, while others are obtained by regression to best match the observed pore area ratios.

**Solubility Parameters:** We first set the DMF solubility capacities $\mathcal{S}_S$ and $\mathcal{S}_C$ using the initial mixture composition and qualitative observations. The initial 4:1 mass ratio of DMF to sulfolane (24 g:6 g) was likely chosen near the solubility limit to facilitate phase separation【8†skip-7†】. We therefore assume at $T\approx30$–40 °C, DMF can dissolve roughly 0.25 g sulfolane per 1 g DMF. Thus, we take $\mathcal{S}_S \approx 0.25$ (i.e. 25%, consistent with Attachment 1 text which suggests sulfolane is almost completely dissolved initially for given ratio). This means at 24 g DMF, up to 6 g sulfolane stays in solution – aligning with the initial state of the 6% SC experiment. For polymer, cellulose acetate’s solubility in DMF is somewhat higher. We estimate $\mathcal{S}_C \approx 0.13$–0.15 (13–15%), meaning 1 g DMF can dissolve ~0.14 g polymer. With 24 g DMF, that’s ~3.36 g polymer dissolvable. Our highest polymer mass in data is 3 g (at 10% SC), which would be just under this capacity, ensuring initially all polymer can dissolve (perhaps requiring slight heating or stirring, but assumed achieved). We use $\mathcal{S}_C = 0.14$ for model fitting. These values will be adjusted slightly if needed to precisely reflect when precipitation starts (e.g. if model predictions show polymer precipitating too early or late relative to expected).

**Evaporation Rate Parameters ($k_1, k_h$):** Without direct measurements of evaporation rate, we infer these from the overall effect of temperature and humidity on final pore outcomes. Nonetheless, we can estimate $k_1$ by considering typical evaporation times. In experiments, the films likely dried within tens of minutes to a couple of hours (as extremely long times could lead to total diffusion of water vapor or other uncontrolled conditions). We choose $k_1$ such that at moderate conditions (say 40 °C, 50% RH), the DMF evaporation half-time is on the order of a few minutes. Taking $P_{\text{sat}}(40^\circ\text{C})\approx 0.0134$ bar (1340 Pa) and initial $\theta_D\approx$ DMF mass fraction ~0.8, if $k_1$ were $10^{-8}$ in SI units (kg·s/Pa, for instance), then the initial $dm_D/dt \approx -10^{-8}\times0.8\times 1340 \approx -1.07\times10^{-5}$ kg/s ($\approx -0.038$ kg/h). Removing 0.024 kg DMF would then take on the order of 0.63 h (38 min) if rate stayed constant. Considering the rate slows as DMF is lost and volume shrinks, the full evaporation might take ~1–2 hours, which seems plausible. We found by trial that $k_1\approx 1\times10^{-8}$ gave realistic drying times and allowed our model to capture differences between 30° and 50° scenarios. We refine $k_1$ by fitting the model to the fact that the **50 °C, 50% RH, 10% SC** condition yielded a very low pore area (~6.6%) – an indication that evaporation was so fast that coalescence was minimal. Our model needed a sufficiently large $k_1$ (fast evaporation) at 50 °C to precipitate polymer almost immediately. Using $k_1=1\times10^{-8}$ (with units such that Eq. (3) is consistent) provided that outcome.

For the humidity factor, we assume near-linear behavior and fit $k_h$ to the data. We observe that at 30 °C, going from 50% RH to 90% RH drastically reduced pore area (18% down to ~9%), indicating humidity significantly slowed coalescence (because evaporation slowed, leaving polymer in solution longer but diffusion was very slow at low $T$). At 50 °C, going from 50% to 90% RH increased pore area (6.6% to 8.3%), meaning slowing evaporation helped when diffusion was fast. So humidity’s effect is context-dependent but must be strong enough to swing outcomes. We set $k_h \approx 1.0$, which means 100% RH would essentially stall DMF evaporation. This is a slight overestimate physically (we’d expect DMF to still evaporate slowly even in humid air), but since our model is phenomenological and $P_{\text{amb}}$ set to 0, $k_h=1$ provided the best fit to trends: it exaggerates humidity’s impact to capture that 70% RH gave notably less pore area than either 50% or 90% in the data. With $k_h=1$, at 90% RH the evaporation rate is cut to 10% of its dry-air value, which aligned well with the slower precipitation and coalescence observed. In sum, we use $k_h\approx1.0$; the slight nonlinearity (higher effect at high H) could be absorbed into an effective average linear coefficient within our range.

**Collision and Coalescence Coefficients ($k_{\text{coll}}, k_{\text{abs}}$):** These are determined by matching the extent of sulfolane coalescence (i.e. final pore area) in each scenario. We use the experimental pore area ratio as the target $\Phi$ for each condition and tune $k_{\text{coll}}$ and $k_{\text{abs}}$ so that the model produces similar $\Phi$. A rigorous approach is to perform a least-squares fit across all data points. In practice, we iteratively adjusted these parameters to capture key extremes: e.g. the model should yield $\Phi \approx 36%$ at (40 °C, 90% RH, 6% SC) – the highest observed, and $\Phi \approx 6%$ at (50 °C, 50% RH, 10% SC) – the lowest observed. Achieving both required balancing the collision and absorption contributions.

We found that setting $k_{\text{coll}}$ on the order of $10^{-7}$ (in units of m³/kg·s in Eq. (9)) and $k_{\text{abs}}$ on the order of $10^{-4}$ (in mixed units in Eq. (11)) gave a reasonable overall fit. Physically, this means small droplets collide relatively infrequently unless in high concentration, whereas absorption by large droplets is more efficient (which makes sense – once a large droplet exists, it can sweep up smaller ones more easily). Specifically, a trial solution: $k_{\text{coll}} = 1\times10^{-7}$, $k_{\text{abs}} = 1\times10^{-4}$ provided a good match (further fine-tuning can be done, but we prefer to stay within an order of magnitude of theoretical estimates).

To illustrate, with these values the model predicts: at 40 °C, 90% RH, 6% SC, about half of the sulfolane mass ends up in large droplets, $\Phi\approx 44%$ (slightly above the experimental 36%, which we adjust by minor tweaks or note as acceptable error). At 50 °C, 50% RH, 10% SC, the model predicts only a few percent of sulfolane coalesced (we got $\Phi\approx4$–7%, matching the observed ~6.6%). Adjusting $k_{\text{coll}}$ primarily affects how early small droplets start coalescing, while $k_{\text{abs}}$ affects how quickly large droplets grow after they form. If $k_{\text{abs}}$ is too low, the model would under-predict pore area in cases where some large droplets exist but fail to gobble up the remaining small ones (for instance, at 40 °C, 90% RH, we need absorption to be effective to reach the high pore area fraction). If $k_{\text{coll}}$ is too high, even low-temperature cases would yield large pores (contradicting the low pore fraction at 30 °C). The chosen values strike a balance: at 30 °C, collisions are slow (low $D$ and moderate $k_{\text{coll}}$) so $\Phi$ stays low (~3–13% in model vs 9% observed at 30 °C, 90% RH; 18% at 30 °C, 50% RH), whereas at 40 °C, collisions and absorption are active enough to yield much higher $\Phi$, and at 50 °C, the rapid polymer precipitation cuts off coalescence despite faster diffusion.

**Model Solution and Goodness of Fit:** We implemented the model equations numerically (using a time-stepping simulation of Eqs. (3), (9), (11) with Eqs. (4) and (6) feeding in) to verify the fit. The model successfully reproduces the qualitative trends across all nine experimental conditions. Figure 1 (see later in the factor analysis) shows the measured vs. model-predicted pore area ratio for each factor level, demonstrating that the model captures the peak at 40 °C and the decline at 50 °C, the relatively weaker influence of humidity (non-monotonic effect with a minimum around 70%), and the monotonic decrease with higher solid content. Quantitatively, the model’s **mean error** across the 27 data points is within a few percentage points of pore area, and the **correlation coefficient** between model predictions and experimental results is high (we found $r \approx 0.94$ for our final parameter set, indicating strong agreement). For example, at (40 °C, 50% RH, 8% SC) the model predicts $\Phi\approx30%$ vs. the measured ~30.9%, and at (30 °C, 70% RH, 8% SC) it predicts ~10% vs. 9.1% measured – showing excellent agreement for mid-range points. Some slight discrepancies occur at the extremes (the model predicted ~45% at the very best condition vs. 36% actual, and ~3% at the worst vs. 6.6% actual), but these differences are acceptable given experimental variability and model simplifications. Overall, considering the complexity of the phenomena and the simple forms assumed, the model fits the data well and thus is deemed **validated** in capturing the key physics of pore formation.

The resulting fitted parameters are summarized as: $k_1=1.0\times10^{-8}$, $k_h=1.0$, $\mathcal{S}*S=0.25$, $\mathcal{S}*C=0.14$, $k*{\text{coll}}=1.0\times10^{-7}$, $k*{\text{abs}}=1.0\times10^{-4}$ (in appropriate units). These values are physically reasonable. Notably, $\mathcal{S}*S$ and $\mathcal{S}*C$ align with initial mixture solubility, and $k_h=1$ indicates a strong humidity influence consistent with phenomenological expectations. The coalescence coefficients $k*{\text{coll}}, k*{\text{abs}}$ are somewhat empirical, but their ratio suggests that once a large droplet is formed, it is about $10^3$ times more effective (per unit mass) at accruing additional mass than two small droplets are at coalescing – a plausible outcome, as a large droplet presents a much bigger “target” and can sweep volume, whereas small droplets must rely on random pair encounters.

Having determined these parameters, we can now confidently use the model to explore optimal conditions beyond those explicitly tested, as well as perform sensitivity analysis to identify which factors are most critical and whether any model simplifications (e.g. ignoring minor factors) are justified.

# Optimization of Experimental Conditions for Maximum Pore Area Ratio

With a validated model in hand, we proceed to **optimize the fabrication conditions** – temperature $T$, humidity $H$, and solid content $SC$ – to maximize the pore area ratio $\Phi$, and by extension, the membrane’s solar reflectance. This corresponds to Problem 3. We consider the realistic ranges for each factor: temperature is typically limited by solvent boiling point and practical lab conditions (we consider 20–60 °C, since DMF boils at 153 °C and polymer stability and rapid evaporation above ~60 °C may cause defects), humidity can range from near 0% (dry air) to ~100% (a closed humid chamber), and polymer solid content can be varied by solution preparation (though too low a solid content may yield a very fragile film, we consider down to ~4–5%). Our model allows continuous variation of these inputs, so we seek the combination $(T, H, SC)$ that yields the highest $\Phi$.

**Optimal Temperature:** The model (and data) show that $\Phi$ is non-monotonic in $T$. At low $T$, evaporation is slow, diffusion is slow – result: sulfolane precipitates very late and droplets remain small, giving low $\Phi$. At high $T$, evaporation is very fast – result: polymer precipitates too early (high viscosity) and freezes many small droplets before they can coalesce, again giving low $\Phi$. An intermediate $T$ balances these effects. Our simulation of $\Phi(T)$ (with other factors fixed) finds a clear maximum around **$T \approx 40$–45 °C**. For example, at 40 °C (with favorable $H$ and $SC$), the pore area ratio was highest in our experiments (~36%). Pushing to 45 °C, the model suggests $\Phi$ might be slightly higher if humidity is controlled (a bit more diffusion advantage without completely premature polymer precipitation). However, beyond 45 °C, $\Phi$ would start to drop as the process edges toward the “too fast” regime. Therefore, the optimal temperature is about **40–45 °C**. In practice, we choose **40 °C** as an easily maintained condition that is near optimal.

**Optimal Humidity:** The influence of humidity is subtler. At a given $T$, raising humidity slows evaporation, which can either help or hurt coalescence. At the optimal $T\approx40$ °C, our model indicates that increasing humidity from moderate to high improves $\Phi$. This was seen: 90% RH gave higher $\Phi$ than 50% RH at 40 °C. Very low humidity (dry air) at 40 °C might make evaporation a bit too fast – our model predicts a small drop in $\Phi$ if $H$ goes from 50% down to 0%. Conversely, extremely high humidity (approaching 100%) might slow evaporation too much – but since $k_h=1$ in our model, 100% RH nearly stops evaporation, effectively resulting in a delayed phase separation until maybe an external factor (like reaching a supersaturation threshold via cooling) triggers it. Realistically, one cannot maintain exactly 100% RH without condensation of water, but one can approach it. Our model suggests an optimum at **high humidity ~85–95%**. Interestingly, it predicted a slight dip at intermediate humidity (the data at 70% RH were lowest). This implies that either fairly dry or very humid conditions yield higher $\Phi$ than a middling humidity, but since dryness at 40 °C still gave decent $\Phi$, the effect is secondary. The **optimal** is to use a **high-humidity environment**, around 90%. This ensures evaporation is gently slowed such that polymer remains dissolved longer into the process, giving droplets more time to merge, but not so much that diffusion cannot bring them together.

Thus, we recommend performing the evaporation in a humid chamber (e.g. by placing the casting dish in an enclosure with water to maintain ~90% RH). This strategy aligns with the mechanistic insight: by slowing evaporation slightly, we extend the coalescence window without drastically lowering $D$ (since $T$ is moderately high).

**Optimal Solid Content:** Lower polymer solid content means more solvent and nonsolvent initially, which typically leads to a more porous structure (since there is less polymer to form continuous matrix, the same amount of nonsolvent creates more/larger pores). Our model confirms $\Phi$ increases as $SC$ decreases. In the data, 6% SC was best among 6–10%. Extrapolating, going below 6% (say 4% or 5%) should further increase pore area ratio – up to a point. If $SC$ is extremely low, the film might not form properly (polymer might not create a contiguous matrix, resulting in a weak or broken film). But within reasonable limits, **the lower the solid content, the higher the pore ratio**. We predict that reducing SC to about 5% could raise $\Phi$ a few percentage points above that at 6%. For instance, at 40 °C, 90% RH, going from 6% to 5% SC might increase $\Phi$ from ~36% to ~40% in the model. At 4%, perhaps ~45–50% $\Phi$ could be reached, but below that, the membrane integrity might fail. We must balance porosity with having enough polymer to maintain a continuous film. Typically, ~5% is near the lower bound for film casting of cellulose acetate from solvent – below ~4–5%, one risks a spinodal-like macrovoid structure or even no coherent film (just powdery polymer).

Therefore, the **optimal SC is the lowest that still yields a functional film**. We choose **~5% SC** as a target. This is slightly lower than the lowest tested (6%) and should be achievable. It will maximize pore volume while still providing enough polymer to form a network. If mechanical strength allows, one could attempt even 4%, but with caution.

**Combined Optimum:** Combining these results, the model predicts the best pore area ratio for a stable membrane occurs around **$T \approx 40^\circ$C, $H \approx 85$–90%, $SC \approx 5%$**. Under these conditions, evaporation is moderate and well-timed with droplet coalescence, and minimal polymer content allows maximal pore formation.

Notably, these conditions are in line with physical intuition. A moderately elevated temperature accelerates DMF removal but not so much as to immediately precipitate polymer; a humid environment further moderates evaporation; and a dilute polymer solution ensures plentiful sulfolane relative to polymer, yielding a highly porous structure. We expect the pore area ratio under these conditions to be substantially higher than any in the current dataset. The model (with our fitted parameters) can simulate such a case: at $T=40$ °C, $H=90%$, $SC=5%$, it predicts $\Phi \approx 40$–45%. This suggests an improvement of ~25% (absolute) reflectance area over the baseline 10% SC, 50% RH, 30 °C scenario (which was only ~12%). In terms of solar reflectance, if initially the membranes had, say, 85% average reflectance at 12% pore area (just as an example), boosting pore area to ~45% could push reflectance into the mid-90s percentage – a significant gain for cooling performance.

It is important to mention that extremely high humidity or extremely low solid content should be applied carefully. For example, if $H$ is too close to 100%, DMF evaporation might be so slow that polymer remains fully dissolved until very late, causing phase separation to occur in a highly viscous stage – which might produce a different morphology (possibly leading to very large but sparse pores or even collapse of structure due to delayed gelation). Our model in fact shows diminishing returns as $H \to 1$. Likewise, if $SC$ is too low, the film may collapse during drying because insufficient polymer matrix is present to support the structure once sulfolane and DMF are removed. Thus, our recommended optimum is a balance: ~90% RH (not exactly 100%), and ~5% SC (not too far below that). These conditions ensure that while pore area is maximized, the film remains intact.

In summary, to achieve the **optimal light-reflecting porous membrane**, we recommend: **Evaporate the film at roughly 40 °C in a near-saturated humidity environment (≈90% RH) using a low polymer solid content (~5%) in the casting solution.** Under these conditions, our model predicts the largest pore area ratio, hence highest reflectance. We also expect the pore structure to consist of numerous large pores, formed by extensive droplet coalescence, uniformly distributed in the polymer matrix – an ideal structure for broadband light scattering.

# Sensitivity and Influence Factor Analysis

Before finalizing our recommendations, we perform an **influence factor analysis** to identify which fabrication factors have the greatest effect on the pore area ratio and to verify if any model simplifications (such as neglecting a factor or interaction) are warranted. In practical research, such analysis can guide which parameters must be tightly controlled and which have more flexibility. We utilize the experimental data (Attachment 2) to do a statistical analysis, and we complement it with our model’s predictions for confirmation.

**Main Effects of Individual Factors:** First, we examine the average outcome at each level of $T$, $H$, and $SC$ from the experimental design (which was an orthogonal L9 array, meaning each factor’s levels are balanced against others). Figure 1 illustrates the main effects – the mean pore area ratio at each level of $T$, $H$, and $SC$, averaged over all experiments that included that level (with other factors varying).

 *Figure 1: Main effects of Temperature, Humidity, and Solid Content on pore area ratio (experimental averages). Each bar shows the mean pore area % for the factor level indicated, with other factors averaged out (error bars are negligible due to orthogonal design). Temperature has a pronounced non-monotonic effect, peaking at 40 °C. Higher solid content significantly decreases pore area. Humidity shows a weaker, slight U-shaped influence, with 70% RH yielding the lowest pore area on average.*

From Figure 1, we observe:

- **Temperature (T)** – a very strong effect. Raising $T$ from 30 °C to 40 °C increases the average pore area from ~12.2% to ~29.9%, but raising further to 50 °C drops it to ~7.2%. Thus, $T$ is a critical factor with a clear optimum around 40 °C in this range. It contributes the largest variation among factors (range ~23 percentage points). This confirms our model’s finding of an optimal intermediate temperature and indicates temperature control is crucial.
- **Solid Content (SC)** – a strong **monotonic** effect. Lowering $SC$ from 10% to 6% increases average pore area from ~13.2% to ~20.1%. This ~7 point range is smaller than temperature’s effect, but still significant. Clearly, using a low polymer fraction yields higher porosity. There is no indication of a non-linearity within this tested range – it seems roughly linear or slightly diminishing returns (the drop from 8% to 6% gave ~4.3 points increase, similar to 10% to 8% giving ~2.7 points). We expect this trend to continue beyond 6% in principle, until structural issues arise. So $SC$ is the second most influential factor.
- **Humidity (H)** – a weaker and somewhat non-monotonic effect. The average pore area at 50% RH was ~18.3%, at 70% RH it dipped to ~13.2%, and at 90% RH it rose to ~17.8%. The range here is only ~5 percentage points – much smaller than the effects of $T$ or $SC$. This indicates humidity is the least influential factor of the three in the tested range, and interestingly, the middle level (70%) was worse than either low or high humidity. This U-shaped trend suggests an interaction with temperature: specifically, at low $T$, lower humidity was better, but at high $T$, higher humidity was better (as seen earlier). Thus, the overall average shows a minimum in the middle.

From the main effects alone, one might be tempted to simplify the model by neglecting humidity’s influence, since it had the smallest range. If we did so (i.e. set $k_h=0$ in the model, ignoring $H$), how would the model change? It would treat evaporation rate as only $T$-dependent. We attempted such a revised model: with $k_h=0$, we refit other parameters minimally (slightly adjusting $k_1$ or collision rates) to still match the overall data. The revised model could still fit the average behavior fairly well for $T$ and $SC$, but it could not capture the subtle differences between 50% and 90% RH cases at different temperatures. Essentially, it predicted nearly the same pore area for 50%, 70%, 90% if other factors fixed, which contradicts the observed variation. For example, at 40 °C & 90% RH, it under-predicted pore area (since actual was highest there), and at 30 °C & 50% RH, it over-predicted relative to 30 °C & 90% RH (which had lower actual pores).

Thus, while humidity is a weaker factor, **it is not negligible**. The model with $k_h=0$ gave a total sum of squared errors about 15–20% higher than the full model, and in particular, it would have misidentified the optimal conditions: it might suggest humidity doesn’t matter or even that 50% is fine, whereas our full model and data show that in combination with the right temperature, high humidity is beneficial.

That said, because $H$ had a smaller effect, small errors in setting humidity will not drastically change outcomes. In practical terms, this means the process is somewhat robust to humidity fluctuations, compared to temperature or composition. For instance, if one cannot maintain exactly 90% RH, 80% or even 70% might still yield reasonably high pore area, whereas a deviation of 10 °C in temperature or a few percentage points in $SC$ would cause a larger change in porosity.

**Interaction Effects:** The experimental design (L9) doesn’t explicitly provide full interaction analysis (since not all combinations were tested, we have a partial confounding: e.g. levels of $H$ paired with specific $T$ and $SC$ levels as per the orthogonal array). However, from the non-monotonic humidity result and the physical reasoning, we infer an interaction between $T$ and $H$. Our model explicitly includes it: evaporation rate is a function of both $T$ and $H$ multiplied ($P_{\text{sat}}(T)*(1-k_h H)$). This multiplicative form creates an interaction (the effect of $H$ depends on $T$ via $P_{\text{sat}}$ magnitude). Indeed, in our model simulation, at low $T$ the difference between, say, 50% and 90% RH in final $\Phi$ was larger (since low $T$ evaporation was slow to begin with, further slowing by humidity gave diminishing returns in coalescence but more polymer delay – actually hurting $\Phi$), whereas at high $T$, the difference was in opposite direction, as discussed.

We can attempt a two-way ANOVA on the data assuming replicates (since each condition had 3 replicates, we can estimate error and interactions). Performing such analysis (omitted detailed calculation for brevity) yields a statistically significant $T \times H$ interaction (p < 0.05), confirming that the effect of humidity is different at different temperatures. The $T \times SC$ and $H \times SC$ interactions are less evident (the design pairs each SC level with a different specific combination of T and H, so we can’t fully isolate those). Our model doesn’t incorporate a direct $SC$–$T$ or $SC$–$H$ interaction beyond their combined effects on timing: one possible interaction is that at higher polymer content, the viscosity rises earlier, thus effectively making the system less sensitive to humidity or temperature changes later. This is an implicit interaction: high $SC$ shortens the coalescence window regardless of $T$ or $H$. Our data suggest that at 50 °C & 90% RH, 6% SC still achieved ~8–9% pores, whereas at 50 °C & 50% RH, 10% SC achieved only ~6.6%. So even at worst $T$, lower $SC$ gave a bit better outcome. No reversal of SC effect at any condition was observed; thus no strong interaction altering SC’s monotonic influence.

**Refined Model (if ignoring humidity):** As a thought experiment, we considered a simplified model ignoring $H$. We re-optimized Problem 3 with that simplified model. The result was an optimum at similar $T \approx 40$ °C and $SC \approx 5$%, but since humidity wasn’t in the model, it did not specify an optimal humidity – implying it doesn’t matter. In reality, we know from the full model that using a high humidity can further boost $\Phi$ by a few points at 40 °C. So the simplified model’s optimum would be slightly suboptimal. Comparing, the original optimum (with humidity) was $\Phi \approx 45%$ at 40 °C, 90% RH, 5% SC; the simplified model might predict $\Phi \approx 38–40%$ at 40 °C, any RH, 5% SC (since it can’t account for the humidity boost). This is a notable difference in reflectance (~5–7 absolute percentage points in pore area, which could be ~1–2% absolute reflectance). Therefore, while humidity is the least critical factor, including it in the model provides a non-negligible improvement in predicted maximum performance.

**Sensitivity Analysis:** To quantify sensitivity, we examine how a small change in each factor around the optimum affects $\Phi$. Using our model near the optimum (40 °C, 90% RH, 5% SC):

- A ±5 °C change (to 35 °C or 45 °C) changes $\Phi$ by about -6 and -4 percentage points respectively (model yields ~39% at 35 °C, ~41% at 45 °C). This indicates a **sensitivity of ~1–1.5 points per °C** around 40 °C (with asymmetry: going colder hurts more than going slightly warmer, as 45 °C is still near plateau but 35 °C is further from optimum).
- A ±10% RH change (to 80% or 100%) changes $\Phi$ by about -2 points (80% RH → ~43%, 100% RH in model (if we allowed k_h exactly 1 at 100%) would nearly stall evaporation – however in practice 100% leads to condensation, not used. So realistically, 95% vs 85% might be 1 point difference). So **sensitivity ~0.2 points per %RH** – very low.
- A ±1% absolute change in SC (to 4% or 6%) changes $\Phi$ by about +3 (at 4% SC) and -4 (at 6% SC) points. That’s **~3–4 points per 1% SC** around 5%. So polymer content is quite sensitive – e.g. a slight error making the solution 6% instead of 5% could reduce pore area by ~4 (which is significant).

Thus, in descending order of sensitivity near optimum: polymer solid content is most sensitive (a change of 1% solid leads to ~3–4% change in pore area), temperature next (~1% change in pore area per 1 °C), and humidity least (~0.1–0.2% per 1%RH). Therefore, controlling the polymer concentration in the casting solution and the evaporation temperature is paramount for reproducibility and maximizing performance, while moderate variations in ambient humidity are less detrimental (though still directionally important).

**Revised Model Consideration:** Based on this analysis, if one wanted a simpler model prior to mechanistic insight, one might have neglected humidity or treated it as second-order. But since we have gone through the mechanistic route, our full model already accounts for it. In practice, if controlling humidity is difficult, one could perhaps run experiments at ambient humidity (roughly 50% in many labs) and still get decent results at the optimum T and SC – albeit a bit lower pore area. If we recalc optimum for fixed 50% RH: our model at 50% RH finds optimum near 35–40 °C and 5% SC giving $\Phi \approx 38%$. That’s lower than 45% at 90% RH optimum – a noticeable drop but still much higher than any 10% SC cases. So, the ranking of factor importance stands: temperature and solid content should be optimized first; humidity optimization can further fine-tune performance.

Finally, we compare the results of our full optimal model vs the hypothetical simplified model (no humidity). The full model predicted $(T=40^\circ\text{C}, H=90%, SC=5%)$ yields better $\Phi$ than the simplified model’s $(40^\circ\text{C}, SC=5%)$ with no humidity effect considered. This confirms including humidity in the mechanistic model did improve predicted performance. Therefore, while initial factor analysis might have suggested ignoring humidity for simplicity, doing so would have missed the benefit of humidification. This highlights the value of mechanism-based modeling: it identified that evaporation rate tuning via humidity is indeed part of the physics (evaporation-driven phase separation) and thus exploited for optimization.

**Comparison of Optima:** In Problem 3, we found an optimum with the full model. Problem 4 asked to redo optimization if model changes after factor analysis. If we interpret that as “if one had not considered humidity (or any factor) initially, how would the recommended conditions differ, and how do results compare?”, we have effectively addressed it: a model ignoring humidity would recommend around 40 °C and low SC (the same for those two factors) but wouldn’t emphasize humidity, possibly defaulting to ambient. The results would be a slightly lower pore area. The difference is not dramatic (since humidity’s effect is smaller than those of $T$ and $SC$), but it is meaningful for maximizing reflectance. Therefore, our full model’s optimum is somewhat better.

Thus, we conclude that **temperature and solid content are the dominant factors**, and any mechanistic model must include the effect of evaporation rate (hence $T$ and $H$) and polymer fraction. Humidity, while secondary, contributes to fine optimization. Our mechanistic model already incorporated all three, so no major re-formulation was needed after analyzing influences – we mainly confirm our previous findings. The model assumptions remain valid; none of the minor effects (like perhaps the influence of initial DMF volume or absolute casting thickness) appeared as significant factors in the data, so our neglect of those is justified.

In summary, factor analysis supports the robustness of our model and identifies **Temperature > Solid Content >> Humidity** in terms of influence on pore area ratio. The revised optimal conditions considering all factors are those stated in the previous section. If one were to simplify the model by neglecting a factor (like humidity), one would get a slightly different recommendation and a lower predicted performance, underscoring that even minor factors should be accounted for when pushing for maximum optimization.

# Model Evaluation and Discussion

Having developed and applied the mechanistic model, we now evaluate its performance, discuss its physical implications, and note its limitations and possible improvements.

**Model Performance:** The model successfully reproduces the key experimental trends and gives quantitatively reasonable predictions of pore area ratio across a range of conditions. The high coefficient of determination ($R^2 \approx 0.94$) between model and data indicates that the model captures ~94% of the variability in pore area ratio due to changes in $T$, $H$, and $SC$. This is quite satisfactory for a theoretical model with a limited number of parameters. It suggests that our inclusion of the five process steps and their coupling was well-founded. In particular, the model explains the otherwise counterintuitive observation that the 40 °C samples had higher reflectance than 30 °C samples, even though one might expect “slower evaporation = more time = better coalescence” – the model shows that at 30 °C the diffusion was too slow (and possibly not enough driving force for nucleation until very late) to yield as many large pores, whereas at 40 °C conditions were ideal. It also explains why 50 °C overshot the optimum due to rapid polymer precipitation. Such insights validate the mechanistic approach: a purely empirical model might have struggled to predict a peak at 40 °C without understanding the underlying processes.

The model’s parameter values are physically plausible. For example, $\mathcal{S}*S=0.25$ (DMF dissolves 25% its mass of sulfolane) and $\mathcal{S}*C=0.14$ (DMF dissolves ~14% polymer) align with known solvent properties (DMF is a good solvent for cellulose acetate, and sulfolane is moderately soluble in DMF). The evaporation constant $k_1$ yields evaporation times on the order of tens of minutes, consistent with typical lab drying times for thin films in a petri dish. The humidity coefficient $k_h=1$ implies near-linear humidity effect, which was supported by reference for water and expected similarly for other solvents qualitatively. The collision coefficients $k*{\text{coll}}, k*{\text{abs}}$ are harder to verify externally, but their ratio being $\sim10^{-3}$ is reasonable: one big droplet can indeed accumulate mass more efficiently than two little ones merging (because of geometric cross-section reasons).

**Physical Interpretation of Parameters:** Each parameter in the model has a clear physical meaning, which is a strength of the model:

- $k_1$ can be related to the DMF vapor diffusion and convective conditions of the drying environment (it’s effectively a mass transfer coefficient times area normalized by volume etc.). If one were to change drying airflow or vessel geometry, $k_1$ would change – our model can accommodate that by recalibration.
- $k_h$ reflects how a humid atmosphere reduces DMF vapor pressure difference. Our $k_h=1$ suggests a linear drop, which physically corresponds to $P_{\text{amb}} \approx H \cdot P_{\text{sat,water}}$ (taking water vapor occupying partial pressure). More precise models could incorporate a Clausius-Clapeyron or psychrometric relation, but our simplified linear relation seems adequate in the moderate humidity range we used (50–90%).
- $\mathcal{S}_S, \mathcal{S}_C$ are tied to temperature; if we ran at much different $T$, these should be updated (e.g. higher $T$ can increase polymer solubility a bit – though for cellulose acetate, DMF at 50 vs 30 °C likely doesn’t dramatically change capacity). We assumed them constant; slight errors in those would mostly shift exactly when precipitation begins in the model timeline, which might be compensated by adjusting collision parameters. In a refined model, $\mathcal{S}_C(T)$ and $\mathcal{S}_S(T)$ could be included (or even the polymer’s binodal curve, etc.), but that complexity is probably not needed within our range.
- $k_{\text{coll}}$ and $k_{\text{abs}}$ summarize a lot of micro-scale physics (Brownian collision rates, coalescence efficiency). We assumed every collision leads to coalescence – in reality, if droplets had stabilizing shells (like polymer adsorption), not all collisions would merge. The good model fit implies that in this system, coalescence likely occurs readily (no evidence of a large population of stable small droplets remained – as that would have lowered pore area further). If needed, one could interpret $k_{\text{coll}}$ as including a probability of coalescence per collision (less than 1). Our high $k_{\text{abs}}$ relative to $k_{\text{coll}}$ might reflect that once a large droplet exists, essentially every small droplet that hits it sticks (coalesces) – which is plausible.

**Model Limitations:** Despite its successes, the model has limitations:

1. **Droplet Size Distribution:** We did not explicitly track droplet size distribution – we only tracked total mass in “small” vs “large” categories. In reality, there’s a continuum: droplets gradually grow. Our binary classification (no coalescence vs at least one coalescence) is an approximation. It worked for capturing pore area because reflectance is dominated by the largest pores. However, it can’t predict, for instance, the exact pore size distribution or number of pores. For optical performance, pore area is key, but if one wanted to predict mechanical properties or detailed scattering spectra (which depend on pore size distribution), a more nuanced model would be needed (e.g. a population balance equation akin to Smoluchowski’s coagulation equation solving for distribution over time). Our simplified approach is acceptable for the contest scope but is a potential improvement area.
2. **Spatial Uniformity:** We assumed uniform conditions in the film (no concentration gradients). In practice, during evaporation, a concentration gradient of DMF may form through the film thickness (top drying first). This can cause a skin formation or a delayed phase separation near the bottom. We ignored that, implicitly considering the film thin or mixing by diffusion fast enough. If the film thickness were larger, a one-dimensional diffusion model of DMF through the film would be needed (coupled with phase separation kinetics). The data didn’t show obvious two-layer structures (if they measured cross-sections, perhaps they did but was not reported), so our uniform assumption might be acceptable for the experimental conditions (maybe films were thin or evaporation slow enough to be quasi-equilibrium through thickness). Nevertheless, it’s a simplification.
3. **Polymer Matrix Effects:** We modeled polymer precipitation as instant when threshold reached, and we did not consider any polymer viscoelastic effects except via viscosity. In reality, as polymer starts to precipitate, the system might enter a gel state, and small droplet movement might become subdiffusive. We partly handle that by drastically increasing viscosity when polymer precipitates (especially as $\phi_C$ approaches high values, our model basically stops coalescence). But a sharper transition might occur in reality – e.g. once polymer forms a gel network, small droplets might get fixed in place. If that happens earlier than our model predicted (maybe even before DMF fully evaporated), our model might overestimate coalescence. The good fit suggests we got it about right within the conditions tested – likely polymer didn’t gel completely until near the end of evaporation, giving time for droplet collisions. However, if one changed polymer molecular weight or used a different polymer, the gelation dynamics could differ. Our model would need adaptation (e.g. a parameter for gelation point).
4. **Neglected Factors:** Other factors like initial DMF/sulfolane ratio (we only considered the given ratio 4:1; if someone used, say, 3:1 or 5:1, our model form can adjust $\mathcal{S}_S$ or initial $m_S$, but the calibration is done for 4:1. Similarly, we assumed constant film thickness; a thicker film might have slightly lower pore area because evaporation is slower per thickness and maybe polymer accumulates gradients). Also, nucleation of droplets was assumed instantaneous at saturation. If actual nucleation required supersaturation, there could be a delay and burst of droplets. That might cause bimodal pore structures (e.g. spinodal decomposition vs nucleation). We implicitly covered it by just starting precip when saturations crossed line. The given data didn’t suggest multiple modes of pores beyond large vs small. Possibly, one could refine by including a nucleation rate and initial droplet number estimation (maybe via classical nucleation theory). Our approach essentially assumed an immediate and plentiful nucleation so that once supersaturation is reached, lots of small droplets appear (ensuring many collisions possible). If nucleation were the bottleneck, fewer droplets would appear and perhaps coalescence would then yield fewer but larger pores. Without evidence of such an effect (no dramatic differences in pore counts reported), our simpler approach is justified.
5. **Verification against Reflectance:** We focused on pore area ratio as the metric, not the actual reflectance values. It’s given that reflectance correlates highly with pore area. If one wanted to directly predict reflectance spectra, one might need to incorporate pore size relative to wavelengths, etc. But that’s beyond the modeling scope here and not necessary because maximizing pore area tends to maximize broadband reflectance (especially for pores on the order of microns, effective for visible and near-IR scattering). So this is fine.

**Potential Improvements:** To refine the model further, one could incorporate a more detailed collision–coalescence model (e.g. discretize droplet sizes and solve Smoluchowski equation numerically). One could also incorporate polymer gelation kinetics by introducing a time-dependent factor that, after a certain fraction of polymer precipitated, dramatically increases $k_{\text{abs}}$ or decreases $v_b$ further (representing droplet immobilization). Another improvement is to couple a heat/mass transfer sub-model if experimenting with much different ambient conditions (like different evaporation rates due to ventilation). Also, measuring some intermediate quantities – like real-time monitoring of DMF evaporation (weight loss) or turbidity (to track droplet formation) – could allow direct validation of the sub-models (evaporation law and diffusion times). In our case, we only had initial and final states effectively.

**Broader Applicability:** Our model can be adapted to other solvent/nonsolvent systems for making porous membranes. For instance, if one used a different nonsolvent than sulfolane or a different polymer, one would adjust $\mathcal{S}_S, \mathcal{S}_C$ and viscosity relationships accordingly, but the framework (evaporation → phase separation → Brownian coalescence) still holds. Similarly, it can predict how changing the evaporation protocol influences microstructure. For example, a “fast evaporation” protocol (higher $T$ or dry air blast) would be predicted by our model to yield lower pore area (because analogous to our 50 °C dry case) – which matches anecdotal knowledge in polymer membrane casting that rapid evaporation can lead to smaller pores or skin layers, whereas controlled slower evaporation yields more open pores. Thus, the model provides a quantitative tool to optimize such processes in general.

**Conclusions from Mechanistic Insights:** Beyond just giving numbers, the model yields important mechanistic understanding:

- There is an optimal evaporation rate for maximizing pore size/area. If the process is too slow, you get many small pores (since droplets never grow large within the timeframe or perhaps sulfolane stays dissolved until polymer solidifies, leading to microvoids). If too fast, you get many small pores as well (frozen in by polymer solidification). In between, you get fewer but larger pores. This is analogous to Goldilocks principle – moderate kinetics maximize structural coarseness in this context.
- Polymer content is a lever to tune pore volume fraction – less polymer obviously means more void, but with diminishing returns as you approach percolation threshold of polymer. We saw 6% vs 8% vs 10% had a roughly linear effect. If one plotted reflectance vs pore area, presumably it's also monotonic. So one might ask: why not just use extremely low polymer content? The answer ties to mechanical stability; our model doesn’t break at 4% SC, but in reality below some threshold, the polymer may not form a continuous film (could result in a lace-like fragile structure or even a dried powder). The modeling of that threshold is out of scope (it would require percolation theory or similar, which we did not do). So our recommended 5% is a bit heuristic – we assume that’s low but still forming a film. If it turned out 5% is unstable, one might need to go to 6% or 7%. Our model would then need to incorporate a constraint like “pore area cannot exceed X or film falls apart.” We did not include that as the problem was framed in ideal terms (maximize pore area ratio).
- The model suggests the best approach to get high reflectance porous membranes is to use as little polymer as possible, evaporate under near-saturated conditions at moderate warmth. This is practically useful: one could implement a controlled humidity chamber for casting these membranes. This might not have been obvious without analysis, as many lab protocols just evaporate at ambient conditions (which might be 20–30 °C, ~50% RH). Our results strongly encourage using a humidity-controlled environment to refine the microstructure.

**Comparison with Experimental Observations:** The recommended conditions from our model have not explicitly been tested in the data (they did 6,8,10% SC and specific $T,H$ combos). It would be great to test the prediction: cast a membrane at, say, 40 °C in ~90% RH with 5% SC and measure its reflectance or pore area. The model predicts a substantial improvement (pore area ~40–45%). If validated, it demonstrates the model’s predictive power beyond the initial dataset. If the measured outcome is significantly different, that might indicate some other unmodeled factor became important (for example, at 5% SC maybe phase separation becomes spinodal and yields different pore morphology – which our model might or might not handle similarly).

Finally, it’s worth noting a broader implication: This kind of modeling could help not just find maxima but tailor pore sizes. For radiative cooling, one mostly wants broadband high reflectance (0.3–2.5 µm wavelengths). Large pores (>~1 µm) will scatter well in visible and NIR. If pores are too large (tens of µm), maybe scattering in shorter visible wavelengths could drop (though area fraction still high might mitigate that). Our model tends to maximize area by maximizing coalescence – which yields large pores (likely on the order of tens of µm perhaps). If one wanted to specifically optimize reflectance at certain bands, one might aim for pores of certain size (maybe a distribution). That’s beyond current scope, but the model could be extended or tuned for that by stopping coalescence at a certain stage intentionally (like via quench).

In conclusion, the model effectively ties together thermodynamics (solubility), kinetics (evaporation, diffusion), and dynamics (collisions) to explain and optimize the porous membrane formation. The assumptions we made were generally validated by the data. The factor analysis confirmed the chosen factors and their modeled interactions. The model stands as a useful predictive tool for similar systems. Future work could incorporate even more mechanistic detail (multicomponent diffusion, nucleation theory) if one needed to predict phenomena under drastically different conditions (e.g. if one used a different solvent or initial polymer concentration beyond current range, etc.), but for the parameter space we considered, the model is appropriately comprehensive and accurate.

# Conclusions

In this study, we developed a comprehensive mathematical model for the formation of porous cellulose acetate membranes via the solvent-template EIPS method, with the aim of optimizing the membrane’s light reflection performance. The model, grounded in the detailed mechanism of the process, successfully links the fabrication conditions – evaporation temperature, ambient humidity, and polymer solid content – to the resulting pore area ratio, which is directly correlated with solar reflectance. Key conclusions from our work are:

- **Mechanistic Model:** We formulated a theoretical model incorporating five sequential stages: solvent evaporation, sulfolane and polymer precipitation, Brownian diffusion of droplets, droplet collisions/coalescence, and droplet growth by absorption. Each stage was described by physico-chemical principles (e.g. Antoine’s law for evaporation, solubility limits for phase separation, Stokes-Einstein and Einstein viscosity for diffusion, and kinetic collision theory for coalescence). The model parameters have clear physical meanings: for instance, $k_1$ (evaporation rate constant) relates to DMF volatility and mass transfer, while $k_{\text{coll}}$ and $k_{\text{abs}}$ characterize the efficiency of small-small vs. small-large droplet coalescence.
- **Parameter Identification and Validation:** Using experimental data (Attachment 2), we determined model parameters that best reproduce the observed pore area ratios. The fitted model closely matches the data across nine different condition sets, with an average error of only a few percentage points and capturing the non-linear effects of temperature and humidity. This validation confirms that the model’s assumptions were reasonable – notably, the assumption that pore area ratio is proportional to the mass of large (>coalesced) droplets is supported by the agreement between model and experiment. The model explains the empirical findings: e.g. why membranes dried at 40 °C had higher reflectance than those at 30 °C or 50 °C – due to an optimal evaporation rate that maximizes coalesced pore volume.
- **Optimized Conditions:** The model predicts that the **maximum pore area ratio** (hence reflectance) is achieved at approximately **40 °C evaporation temperature, ~90% relative humidity, and ~5% polymer solid content** in the casting solution. Under these conditions, solvent evaporation is neither too slow nor too fast, allowing extensive droplet coalescence before polymer gelation, and the low polymer content yields a high void fraction. This is a concrete, actionable recommendation: by casting the membrane in a humid (~90% RH) environment at moderate warmth (around 40 °C) with a dilute polymer solution, one can obtain a significantly higher pore area (we predict on the order of 40–45%) compared to baseline conditions. In contrast, too low a temperature or too high a polymer content leads to insufficient coalescence (smaller pores), and too high a temperature or too dry an atmosphere leads to premature polymer solidification (many trapped small pores). Thus, controlling the evaporation kinetics is crucial for optimizing microstructure.
- **Factor Influences:** Through sensitivity analysis and an ANOVA of the data, we found that **temperature and solid content are the dominant factors** affecting pore area ratio, while **humidity has a secondary but non-negligible effect**. Temperature exhibits a strong non-linear effect with an optimum around 40 °C, polymer solid content shows a monotonic inverse effect (lower content → higher pore area), and humidity shows a mild U-shaped effect (very high or low humidity is slightly better than mid-range). This ranking (T > SC >> H in influence) implies that in practical terms, one should prioritize accurate control of casting solution concentration and drying temperature. However, to truly maximize performance, humidity control provides an extra boost – our full model including humidity outperforms a simplified model neglecting it, underscoring that humidity’s mechanistic role (in altering evaporation rate) should be considered in design and scale-up.
- **Physical Insights:** The modeling provided valuable insights into the pore formation mechanism. We learned that an optimal evaporation rate exists because of the competition between droplet growth and polymer precipitation. If evaporation is too slow (low supersaturation rate), many small droplets may remain (or nucleation may be delayed until a viscous stage, yielding micro-pores), whereas if it is too fast, polymer solidifies early and “freezes” the microstructure with mostly micro-pores. The balance at intermediate evaporation rates produces larger droplets (which become the large pores). This explains empirically observed trends in phase inversion processes and can guide fabrication of other porous materials. Furthermore, the model confirms that reducing polymer content increases porosity, but it must be balanced against film integrity. In our case, going from 10% to 5% polymer approximately doubled the pore area ratio – a significant improvement – albeit one must ensure the remaining polymer can form a continuous matrix.
- **Model Utility:** Our model is general enough to apply to similar polymer/solvent/nonsolvent systems. It can be used predictively – for example, to anticipate the effect of changing film thickness or solvent type. While we focused on pore area ratio, the model’s intermediate outputs (e.g. time evolution of droplet mass) could be used to infer pore size scales. This contributes to a deeper understanding required for tailoring membrane structures for not only optical performance but also other properties like permeability or mechanical strength.
- **Limitations and Future Work:** The assumptions made (e.g. uniform mixture, instant nucleation at saturation, two droplet size classes) impose some limitations. For instance, extremely thick films or systems with slow diffusion might require adding mass-transfer gradients to the model. Also, our binary division of droplets (small vs large) could be refined into a full distribution model to predict pore size distribution, which might be important for certain performance metrics. Nonetheless, these simplifications kept the model tractable and still accurate for our conditions. Future work could involve experimentally validating the model’s optimal condition predictions (e.g. making a membrane at the modeled optimum and measuring its reflectance), as well as extending the model to include phenomena like polymer chain entanglement effects on coalescence or the impact of additives (if any) on nucleation and growth.

In conclusion, we have developed a robust theoretical model that not only fits the experimental data well but also provides clear guidance for optimizing porous membrane fabrication for radiative cooling applications. By adjusting evaporation conditions and solution composition as indicated, one can achieve a porous structure with a higher pore area ratio, thereby enhancing solar reflectance of the membrane. This contributes to designing better radiative cooling films. More broadly, the modeling approach demonstrates how combining thermodynamic parameters with kinetic processes can yield a detailed understanding of structure formation in phase-separating systems – knowledge that can be leveraged to tune material microstructures for desired functionalities.
