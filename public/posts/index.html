Abstract Porous cellulose acetate membranes fabricated via solvent-template evaporation-induced phase separation (EIPS) are promising for passive radiative cooling due to their high solar reflectance. We develop a mechanistic mathematical model to optimize and control the membrane’s light reflection performance, quantified by the pore area ratio in SEM images. The model integrates five sequential process stages – solvent evaporation, nonsolvent and polymer precipitation, Brownian diffusion of nascent droplets, droplet coalescence into larger droplets, and growth of large droplets – each grounded in physical theory. Evaporation kinetics are described by a first-order model incorporating DMF vapor pressure (via the Antoine equation) and a linear humidity suppression term. Phase separation of sulfolane (nonsolvent) and polymer is captured by mass balance constraints using solubility limits. Brownian motion of sulfolane droplets is modeled via the Stokes-Einstein diffusion coefficient, with solution viscosity enhanced by precipitated polymer according to Einstein’s effective viscosity relation. Droplet collision kinetics are formulated for both small-small collisions (creating new large droplets) and small-large collisions (growth of existing large droplets).
